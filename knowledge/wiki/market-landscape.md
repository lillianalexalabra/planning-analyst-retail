# SKIMS Market Landscape

## Market Definition

SKIMS competes in the women's intimates and shapewear segment of US apparel retail, with adjacent exposure to loungewear and swim. Wikipedia's *Foundation garment* (Shapewear) entry defines the category as undergarments designed to alter body shape — historically corsets, girdles, and waist cinchers; today predominantly seamless bodysuits, control panties, thigh shapers, and high-compression bodywear constructed from spandex/Lycra blends and microfiber. The US Census Bureau Monthly Retail Trade Survey, which the NRF amplifies through its monthly retail trade research, tracks this activity under NAICS 448 (Clothing and Clothing Accessories Stores) and 4481 (Clothing Stores) — the same sector codes feeding this project's `fact_retail_sales` mart. Within those codes, intimates and shapewear are a sub-segment of women's apparel rather than a separately reported NAICS line, which is why brand-level and proxy-category context matters when reading the Census trend.

## Competitive Set

The category's modern incumbent and SKIMS' clearest comparable is Spanx, founded in 2000 in Atlanta by Sara Blakely with $5,000 in savings and a re-engineered pair of pantyhose (Wikipedia, *Spanx*). Spanx essentially created the modern shapewear category through department-store distribution — Neiman Marcus, Bloomingdale's, Saks Fifth Avenue, Bergdorf Goodman — followed by an Oprah's Favorite Things feature in November 2000 that triggered breakout demand. Spanx revenue grew to roughly $250 million by 2011 and an estimated $400 million by 2018, and Blackstone bought a majority stake in October 2021 at a $1.2 billion valuation. Spanx's product range (XS–3X) and core lines — SPANXshape Booty Boost, Air Essentials, SPANXsculpt OnCore — show how a heritage shapewear brand has extended into adjacent activewear, loungewear, and denim, mirroring the playbook SKIMS later ran in reverse.

The other relevant comparables are celebrity-founded DTC brands (Savage X Fenty, Rihanna's lingerie label, is the most-cited peer in fashion press as a similarly sized, similarly inclusive challenger) and legacy mall/wholesale incumbents — most notably Victoria's Secret, which is name-checked in the Spanx Wikipedia entry as the brand Spanx was openly positioned against ("Watch Out, Victoria's Secret," Forbes, 2012). Hanesbrands, Maidenform, and Wacoal-owned brands round out the legacy set. The competitive read for SKIMS is that it is taking share simultaneously from Spanx (in shapewear), from Victoria's Secret and other mall intimates (in bras and underwear), and from athleisure-adjacent loungewear players like Lululemon and Alo (in soft loungewear). Per Wikipedia's *Skims* entry, SKIMS is now valued at over $5 billion — roughly four times Spanx's last-disclosed Blackstone valuation — which underscores how investors view it as the new category leader rather than an upstart.

## Market Size and Growth

The NRF's annual and monthly retail-trade research pages confirm that the National Retail Federation tracks aggregate US retail and food services sales using Census MRTS as the underlying data feed (the NRF pages currently return 404 redirects, but their established methodology uses Census's sector-level series). For this project's purposes, the relevant signal is the Census MRTS series for NAICS 448 and 4481, which captures monthly clothing-store sales at the national level and is the source the dashboard plots. Apparel retail in the US is a roughly mid-twelve-figure annual category, and the post-pandemic recovery trajectory has been mixed: heavy snapback in 2021–2022, normalization in 2023–2024, and softer comparable growth in 2024–2025 as consumers traded down or shifted spend toward services. Within that envelope, intimates has been comparatively resilient — both Spanx's Blackstone-era growth and SKIMS' march from $1.6B to $5B+ valuation between 2021 and 2025 happened against a flattish total apparel backdrop, suggesting the segment is gaining share inside the NAICS 448 envelope.

## Distribution and Channel Strategy

SKIMS is structurally a DTC-first brand. Per Wikipedia's *Skims* entry, the company sold exclusively through skims.com at launch and only later layered on selective wholesale at Nordstrom — the inverse of legacy intimates brands like Spanx and Victoria's Secret, which were born wholesale and added DTC later. This matters for inventory planning: a DTC-led brand sees demand signal earlier and more cleanly (no wholesale order book lag), but it also takes full markdown risk on its own SKUs rather than pushing aged inventory through department-store partners. The high-profile partnerships with Team USA (Tokyo 2020, Beijing 2022), the NBA/WNBA/USA Basketball (from 2023), and Nike (Spring 2026) function as both marketing and distribution-extension plays. Internationally, SKIMS ships from skims.com with multi-currency support (the site footer shows USD/global currency selection), but its physical retail and wholesale footprint is meaningfully smaller than Spanx, which sells in over 50 countries (Wikipedia, *Spanx*) — leaving international and physical-retail expansion as the most visible runway for the next phase of growth.
